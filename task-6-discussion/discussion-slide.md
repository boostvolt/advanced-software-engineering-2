This file contains the content for the single discussion slide to be presented after another team’s report. ## Slide Content

### What do we now know that we did not know before?

The report makes a useful contribution by showing that **[insert the most genuinely new insight from the other team’s work]**. The strongest value of this insight is that it extends the discussion beyond general assumptions and makes the issue more concrete from a software-engineering perspective.

### Are the reported conclusions generalizable?

The conclusions appear to be **partially generalizable**. They are most convincing in contexts where **[insert the setting in which the conclusions clearly apply]**. However, their transferability is more limited when the surrounding conditions change, especially with respect to **[insert architecture, team size, regulation, domain, or process constraints]**. In other words, the conclusions are useful, but they should be interpreted with awareness of scope.

### In which way can we relate to the lessons learned?

We can relate strongly to the report’s emphasis on **[insert shared issue, such as context limits, integration difficulty, validation effort, coordination cost, or governance]**. In our own project, we also observed that seemingly strong technical support often remains dependent on hidden assumptions and careful review. This makes the lesson relatable not only at the tool level, but also at the process and decision-making level.

### Controversial question

**What is the most questionable issue of the report?**

The most questionable issue is not necessarily the conclusion itself, but rather **[insert the weakest assumption, insufficient evidence, or methodological overreach]**. The report would become stronger if it clarified **[insert the missing comparison, missing limitation, missing context boundary, or missing evidence]**.

**Alternative controversial option: Why is the experience not very representative?**

The experience may not be fully representative because it is based on **[insert narrow task, specific environment, unusually skilled team, unusually favorable dataset, or limited system context]**. This does not invalidate the result, but it does reduce how broadly the findings can be applied.

### Constructive closing statement

Overall, the report is valuable because it contributes a clear perspective on **[insert the main strength]**. At the same time, its argument would become more robust if **[insert one realistic improvement]** were addressed more explicitly.

## Suggested Oral Delivery

When presenting this slide, keep the tone balanced. Start with the strongest contribution of the other team. Then address generalizability and the controversial question without sounding dismissive. The goal is not to defeat the report, but to show that you can evaluate it critically and fairly.

A good structure is:

First, say what the report added.  
Second, explain where its conclusions do and do not travel well.  
Third, connect one of its lessons to your own project experience.  
Finally, raise one concrete limitation and one constructive suggestion.

This order helps the criticism sound rigorous rather than adversarial.

---

## Mini Checklist Before Presenting

Before finalizing the slide, make sure you can answer the following in one sentence each:

Did we identify one genuinely new insight?  
Did we say where the conclusions are and are not generalizable?  
Did we connect their lessons to our own project?  
Did we choose one controversial question and answer it clearly?  
Did we keep the feedback constructive?

If all five answers are yes, the slide is ready.
